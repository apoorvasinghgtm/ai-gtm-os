# Human vs. AI Responsibility Framework

**Status: Stable**

The most important design decision in any AI-native GTM system is not which tools to use or which workflows to automate. It is knowing precisely where to use AI and where to keep humans in the driver's seat.

Get this wrong in one direction and you automate work that requires human judgment — producing outputs that are technically correct but strategically hollow. Get it wrong in the other direction and you leave humans doing work that AI handles faster, cheaper, and more consistently.

This document defines the line.

---

## The core principle

AI should own work that is **retrievable, repeatable, and research-intensive.**
Humans should own work that is **judgment-dependent, relational, and creatively generative.**

Neither is superior. They are different categories of work that require different capabilities. The goal of an AI-native GTM system is not to automate as much as possible — it is to ensure every unit of human attention is spent where it creates the most irreplaceable value.

---

## AI's lane

### Research and intelligence synthesis
AI processes large volumes of unstructured information faster and more comprehensively than any human. Use it here without hesitation.

- Account research: company news, funding signals, hiring patterns, technology stack
- Competitive intelligence: product changes, positioning shifts, win/loss pattern analysis
- Market signal monitoring: trigger events, intent data, industry developments
- Customer data synthesis: support tickets, usage patterns, NPS themes

**What it produces:** Pre-sale intelligence briefs, competitive snapshots, deal risk alerts, expansion signal reports

**What humans do with it:** Review for relevance, apply deal-specific context, make strategic decisions about what to act on

---

### First-draft generation
AI produces consistent, well-structured first drafts faster than humans, and does so without creative fatigue or blank-page friction. Use it for all high-volume, pattern-driven content.

- Discovery call prep documents
- Personalized outreach and follow-up emails
- ROI models and business case frameworks
- Enablement materials and talk tracks
- Internal briefing documents

**What it produces:** Complete drafts ready for human editing, not raw material requiring heavy rework

**What humans do with it:** Edit for relationship context, sharpen the strategic angle, apply judgment about what to include or cut

---

### Consistency enforcement
AI applies rules without fatigue. Use it to ensure every output reflects the current state of your positioning, ICP, and competitive narrative — not last quarter's version.

- Checking outputs against brand voice rules
- Flagging messages that use deprecated terminology
- Ensuring competitive claims are accurate against the current landscape

**What it produces:** Consistent, on-brand outputs at scale

---

### Administrative work
Any work that is primarily about capturing, formatting, or routing information belongs to AI.

- Call summaries and next action identification
- CRM field updates from call recordings
- Meeting follow-up drafts
- Pipeline data organization

**What it produces:** Time back for humans to spend on relationship and strategy work

---

## Human's lane

### Live relational judgment
No AI can read a room. The instinct that tells you a conversation has shifted, that the champion is losing confidence, that the economic buyer needs to hear something different — that is irreplaceable human capability.

- Reading emotional signals in a live conversation
- Deciding when to slow down, go off-script, or change direction entirely
- Building the kind of trust that comes from genuine presence and attention

**Why AI cannot own this:** It requires real-time, contextual, emotionally intelligent interpretation of human behavior. Current AI has no access to live context and no emotional intelligence.

---

### Strategic framing and prioritization
AI surfaces information. Humans decide what it means and what to do about it.

- Deciding which insight from an intelligence brief actually matters for this specific account and why
- Determining the right strategic play when signals point in different directions
- Knowing when the data-driven recommendation is wrong because of something the data doesn't capture

**Why AI cannot own this:** Strategy requires applying judgment under uncertainty with incomplete information. AI pattern-matches against historical data. Novel situations — which are common in enterprise sales — require reasoning that goes beyond pattern-matching.

---

### Creative leaps
The unexpected angle. The reframe that unlocks a stalled deal. The narrative that positions you as a category of one rather than a better version of a competitor. These require genuinely original thinking.

- Developing positioning that hasn't been tried before
- Finding the non-obvious insight that changes how a buyer thinks about their problem
- Writing the message that earns a response from an executive who deletes everything else

**Why AI cannot own this:** AI optimizes within known solution spaces. Creative breakthroughs require stepping outside them. AI can assist — by generating options, stress-testing ideas, offering analogies — but the generative act requires human creativity.

---

### High-stakes executive conversations
When the conversation is with a C-suite buyer making a $500K+ decision, authenticity, nuance, and presence matter more than any amount of preparation. AI can prepare you for this conversation. It cannot have it for you.

- Executive briefings and QBRs
- Board-level presentations
- Crisis conversations with at-risk customers
- Final-stage negotiations

**Why AI cannot own this:** Trust at this level is built person-to-person. It requires the ability to improvise, demonstrate genuine understanding, and be accountable in ways that AI cannot replicate.

---

### Ethical judgment
When the AI-recommended play feels wrong for the relationship, the customer, or the long-term business — override it.

- Deciding not to send a technically correct message because the timing is wrong
- Recognizing when optimizing for short-term revenue damages long-term trust
- Knowing when a customer's objection is actually feedback you should act on, not a barrier to overcome

**Why AI cannot own this:** Ethics requires values, accountability, and long-term thinking in ways that exceed current AI capability. Humans are accountable for outcomes. AI is not.

---

## The collaborative zone

Some work doesn't belong entirely to AI or humans — it requires both working together, with AI as a thinking partner rather than a producer.

| Task | AI's role | Human's role |
|---|---|---|
| Positioning decisions | Surfaces options, stress-tests against ICP and competitive landscape | Makes the call, owns the outcome |
| Messaging hierarchy | Drafts multiple framings, flags inconsistencies | Decides what's true and what lands |
| Deal strategy | Identifies risk patterns, suggests plays from historical data | Owns the decision, applies relationship context |
| Creative briefs | Structures the brief, generates initial territory | Pushes the idea beyond the obvious |
| Pricing conversations | Models scenarios, surfaces comparable deals | Navigates the negotiation in real time |

---

## How to use this in practice

When designing or evaluating any GTM workflow, run it through three questions:

1. **Is this primarily retrieval, pattern-matching, or generation from available information?** → AI leads.
2. **Does this require live judgment, genuine creativity, or relational trust?** → Human leads.
3. **Does this require surfacing options and then making a strategic call?** → Collaborative zone. Design the handoff explicitly.

The failure mode to avoid is the gray zone by default — where AI does the work but nobody decides whether it was the right work to do. Every workflow should have a named human review gate, and every review gate should have a clear question it is answering.
