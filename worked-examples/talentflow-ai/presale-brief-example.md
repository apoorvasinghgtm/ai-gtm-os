## Step 1 Output: Account Situation Analysis

### Company Snapshot
- Brightfield Analytics is a 1,200-person B2B data and analytics SaaS company, Series D, headquartered in Austin
- Closed a $90M Series D 18 months ago; has grown GTM headcount approximately 40% since then
- Currently hiring aggressively in enterprise sales and solutions engineering — 32 open roles in talent acquisition's portfolio as of last month
- Recent 10-K language shifted from "headcount growth" to "talent quality and retention" — signals a maturation from growth-at-all-costs to efficiency
- Marcus Chen joined 6 months ago from a larger enterprise, likely brought in to build a more sophisticated TA infrastructure

### Trigger Events
- Series D closed 18 months ago — budget has been deployed, now showing signs of optimization focus
- 40% GTM headcount growth in 18 months — ramp quality and consistency is almost certainly a challenge
- Marcus Chen hired 6 months ago — mandate likely includes modernizing TA operations, justifying headcount, and delivering measurable improvement
- 32 open roles currently — active, high-volume hiring in progress
- Downloaded "2024 Recruiter Capacity Benchmarks" — signal of active interest in understanding how they compare to peers on recruiter productivity

### Stakeholder Context (Marcus Chen, VP of Talent Acquisition)
At 6 months in, Marcus is past the "listening tour" phase and likely presenting a roadmap. His success metrics in this role probably include: time-to-hire, offer acceptance rate, recruiter capacity (roles per recruiter), and quality of hire — with increasing scrutiny from the CFO post-Series D on cost-per-hire and recruiter headcount justification. He needs a win that he can point to in his first year, something that demonstrates he's building infrastructure, not just filling roles. He reports to the CHRO, who will want to see ROI tied to business outcomes, not just TA efficiency.

### Timing Risks
- At 6 months, Marcus may still be building political capital — he may not have full budget authority without CHRO approval
- 32 open roles creates urgency, but it also means his team is stretched thin and has limited bandwidth for a new tool implementation
- No signal yet on existing TA tech stack — if they're deeply committed to a manual or legacy process, change management could be a blocker

## Step 2 Output: Pain Point Hypotheses and Discovery Prep

### Three Pain Point Hypotheses (ranked)

**Hypothesis 1 — Recruiter capacity inconsistency (most likely)**
With 40% headcount growth and 32 open roles, Brightfield has more hiring volume than their recruiting team can handle at a consistent quality level. Some recruiters are producing strong slates; others are falling behind or relying on candidates that don't clear hiring manager review. Marcus downloaded our capacity benchmarks guide — he's probably already measuring this gap.
*Confirmation signal:* If he says things like "our time-to-qualified-slate is inconsistent" or "we're losing candidates to slow process."
*Disconfirmation signal:* If he says his team is staffed adequately and the issue is offer acceptance, not slate quality.

**Hypothesis 2 — New rep ramp time for recruiters (moderate likelihood)**
With aggressive hiring across the TA team, newer recruiters may not be sourcing at the same quality level as experienced ones. If Brightfield is using Greenhouse or Lever (both common at their stage), the data exists to show this variance — but without a tool surfacing it, Marcus is managing on intuition.
*Confirmation signal:* If he distinguishes between what his experienced recruiters produce vs. new hires, or if he references onboarding as a challenge.

**Hypothesis 3 — CHRO-level accountability for TA ROI (secondary)**
At Series D, finance is asking harder questions about every function. Marcus likely needs to show cost-per-hire improvement and recruiter utilization data that justifies his team's headcount. This isn't his primary pain right now, but it's the frame that gets budget approved.
*Confirmation signal:* If he mentions board reporting, cost-per-hire targets, or CFO involvement in TA spend.

### Five Discovery Questions

1. "What surprised you most about how the recruiting team operates when you joined six months ago?" *(Opens with their perspective, not our hypothesis)*

2. "When you look at your top two or three recruiters versus the rest of the team — what are they doing differently in how they approach sourcing?" *(Surfaces the consistency problem without telegraphing our solution)*

3. "With 32 open roles right now — how is the team deciding which ones to prioritize, and how is that working?" *(Reveals capacity constraints and prioritization challenges)*

4. "What does your CHRO care most about when it comes to talent acquisition results this year?" *(Identifies the executive success metric that drives budget)*

5. "What would you want to be able to point to at the end of your first year here?" *(Personal and forward-looking — builds trust and surfaces his real mandate)*

### Listening Priorities
- Language he uses to describe his team's challenges: "capacity," "quality," "consistency," "speed" — tells us which hypothesis is closest
- Whether he mentions the CHRO or CFO unprompted — signals how much budget scrutiny he's under
- His tone when describing the current state: resigned vs. energized — signals whether he has executive air cover to make changes

### False Assumptions to Avoid
- **Assuming urgency because of 32 open roles.** High volume hiring can create urgency, or it can create paralysis — "we don't have time to implement a new tool right now." Don't lead with "we know you're busy" framing.
- **Assuming he has full budget authority.** Six months in, CHRO approval is likely required for any new vendor commitment. The champion play here matters.

## Step 3 Output: Competitive Context, ROI Anchor, and Call Opener

### Competitive Context
The most likely alternative at Brightfield is the status quo — recruiters sourcing manually through LinkedIn Recruiter and Greenhouse. At their stage, they may have explored or piloted tools like Beamery or Gem, but a full AI-native sourcing platform is likely new territory.

**Positioning against status quo:**
"Most TA teams at your stage are doing this manually — which works until volume outpaces team capacity. What you've described isn't a people problem; it's an infrastructure problem."

**What not to say:** Do not reference "automating hiring decisions" — Brightfield's CHRO and legal team at this stage will flag any suggestion of autonomous hiring. Our positioning is human-in-the-loop by design, not as a feature addition.

### ROI Anchor
**Best proof point for this conversation:** "3.2x recruiter capacity increase without adding headcount, measured across 180 enterprise customers."

**Why this proof point:** Marcus is under pressure to show ROI for his team's headcount. A 3.2x capacity multiplier directly addresses his CHRO's question of "why do we need more recruiters?" — it's the proof that the right infrastructure lets his current team do more.

**When to introduce it:** After Marcus describes the capacity or consistency challenge in his own words. Not before. Introduce it as: "One pattern we see with TA teams your size — [stat]. What that looked like for [customer type] was [context]."

### Suggested Call Opener
"Marcus, thanks for making time. I saw the capacity benchmarks guide came through — curious what prompted you to look at that. Before I share anything about what we do, I'd love to understand what you're walking into right now in terms of what the team is handling and what you're trying to build."

*(This opener acknowledges the inbound signal, positions the call as diagnostic, and gives him the floor first.)*
