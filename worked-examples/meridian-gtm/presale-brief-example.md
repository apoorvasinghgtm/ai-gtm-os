## PART 1 — Account Situation Analysis

### Company Snapshot

Vantara Systems is an 800-person enterprise technology company selling infrastructure automation software to mid-market and enterprise IT buyers. They closed a $65M Series D 14 months ago and have been in a sales team scaling mode since — headcount in the GTM org grew approximately 35% over the past year based on LinkedIn hiring data. Revenue targets have scaled with headcount, but public commentary from the CEO at a recent industry event referenced "productivity per rep" as the key growth lever for the coming year, signaling that the volume-based hiring phase is over and the efficiency phase is beginning.

*[Trigger: "productivity per rep" language from CEO is a direct buying signal for Meridian. Rep should reference this — it validates the problem without the rep having to sell it.]*

### Trigger Events

| Event | Signal |
|---|---|
| Series D closed 14 months ago | Capital deployed, now accountable for returns. Growth-at-all-costs phase ending. |
| 35% GTM headcount growth in 12 months | Large new rep cohort. Ramp time and consistency are acute problems. |
| Sarah Okonkwo hired 90 days ago | New enablement leader, establishing her agenda. High motivation to show early wins. |
| CEO referenced "rep productivity" publicly | Executive-level framing of the problem. Enablement has air cover to act. |
| Inbound on "Rep Ramp Benchmarks" guide | Sarah is actively researching this problem space. She's not being cold-called into a conversation — she came looking. |

### Stakeholder Context

Sarah is 90 days into her first Head of Sales Enablement role at this company. She likely came in with a mandate to modernize or systematize what was previously ad hoc. At 90 days, she has diagnosed the problems but has not yet shipped a major initiative — she is in the window where she needs a visible win to establish credibility with the VP of Sales who hired her.

Her success metrics are probably defined around rep ramp time, content utilization, and some version of sales productivity improvement — metrics she will need to show movement on within 6 months to validate the hire.

She downloaded a benchmarks guide, not a product overview. She is in research mode, not buying mode. She wants to understand where Vantara stands relative to peers before she decides what to fix and how.

*[This matters for the opening. Don't pitch. She's not ready to evaluate a solution. She's still diagnosing.]*

### Risk Flags

- **No VP of Sales sponsorship confirmed yet.** Sarah is the champion but not the economic buyer. If she loves it but the VP doesn't know it exists, deals stall.
- **90 days in = still building political capital.** She may be cautious about committing to a new vendor before she has full trust from the sales team. Don't push for a fast close.
- **Large new rep cohort may mean budget is already allocated** to onboarding tooling purchased before she arrived. Understand the existing stack before positioning.

## PART 2 — Pain Point Hypotheses and Discovery Prep

### Three Pain Point Hypotheses

**Hypothesis 1 — Ramp time inconsistency [High probability]**
With 35% headcount growth in 12 months, Vantara has a large cohort of reps at different stages of ramp. Hypothesis: some reps are ramping well, most are not, and there's no systematic explanation for why. Sarah has probably been asked "why is rep X ahead of rep Y?" and doesn't have a good answer yet.

*Confirms if:* She mentions variation in ramp time across the cohort, or inability to identify what "good" looks like early.
*Disconfirms if:* She describes a structured onboarding program that's already working and her focus is elsewhere.

**Hypothesis 2 — Prep quality and meeting consistency [Medium-high probability]**
With a fast-growing team, many reps are new to enterprise selling or new to this product and ICP. Hypothesis: meeting quality is inconsistent — some reps walk in prepared, most don't, and it's showing up in conversion rates between first call and demo.

*Confirms if:* She references discovery quality, pipeline conversion rates, or rep preparation as a concern.
*Disconfirms if:* Reps are primarily doing outbound SDR-type work rather than complex enterprise discovery.

**Hypothesis 3 — Enablement content isn't being used [Medium probability]**
New enablement leaders almost always inherit a library of content nobody uses. Hypothesis: Sarah has already found that existing playbooks, talk tracks, and battle cards are out of date or too generic for reps to apply to real accounts.

*Confirms if:* She mentions content utilization, reps not using existing materials, or the need to rebuild the enablement library.
*Disconfirms if:* She describes a well-adopted content system already in place.

---

### Five Discovery Questions

1. *"You've been in the role 90 days — what's the one thing you've seen about how reps prepare for meetings that surprised you most?"*
   → Opens with her experience, not our product. Maps to Hypothesis 2. Non-threatening.

2. *"When a new rep has a great first quarter, what does their preparation and research process tend to look like compared to someone who's struggling?"*
   → Surfaces whether she's already diagnosed the prep quality problem. Maps to Hypotheses 1 and 2.

3. *"With the headcount growth you've had, how consistent is the onboarding experience today — are reps getting to their first deal at a similar pace?"*
   → Direct probe of Hypothesis 1. Gives her permission to name the problem without feeling like she's criticizing her team.

4. *"What does the VP of Sales think the biggest productivity lever is for this year — where have those conversations landed?"*
   → Gets VP context without making her feel bypassed. Critical for understanding whether she has executive air cover.

5. *"What would a successful first six months in this role look like for you — what do you want to be able to point to?"*
   → Maps to her personal success criteria. Tells you what she needs to justify internally, which is what you'll eventually need to deliver.

---

### Listen For

- **Any mention of specific metrics she's being held to** (ramp time, productivity per rep, content utilization) — these become the ROI anchors for your business case
- **How she talks about the VP of Sales** — level of alignment, whether she mentions him/her spontaneously, tone — signals how much internal support she has
- **Whether she uses "we" or "I"** when describing problems — "we" means she has organizational buy-in; "I" may mean she's still diagnosing alone

### Avoid Assuming

- **Don't assume she has budget authority.** She almost certainly doesn't for a $100K+ contract at 90 days in role. Budget is with the VP. Plan for a multi-stakeholder motion from the start.
- **Don't assume the problem is rep preparation.** She downloaded a ramp benchmarks guide — that's the entry point, not the conclusion. The real problem could be content quality, manager coaching, onboarding structure, or something else entirely. Diagnose before proposing.

## PART 3 — Competitive Context and ROI Anchor

### Competitive Posture

No competitor has been mentioned. Based on Vantara's profile and stack signals, the most likely competitive alternatives are:

- **Highspot or Seismic** — content management and enablement platforms. She may already have one. If so, Meridian is not competing with them — we're complementary. Highspot manages content; Meridian synthesizes account intelligence. Clarify this early if it comes up.
- **"We'll build it ourselves"** — common at companies with strong ops teams. Counter: internal build requires ongoing maintenance, context layer management, and prompt governance that most ops teams underestimate.
- **Status quo / doing nothing** — most likely at 90 days. She's still in diagnosis mode. The competitive threat is her deciding to tackle a different problem first.

*[Don't lead with competitive positioning on a first call. Only surface if she raises it.]*

### ROI Anchor

**Most relevant proof point for Sarah:** *41% faster ramp time for new AEs using Meridian vs. control group (onboarding study, 2024)*

**Why it lands for her specifically:** She has a large cohort of recently hired reps, ramp time is her most visible problem, and she needs a metric she can take to the VP. Ramp time is a metric the VP of Sales owns and cares about — this is her internal business case in one number.

**How to introduce it (not as a data dump):**
After she describes the ramp problem in her own words — not before — say: *"That pattern comes up a lot with teams that have scaled headcount quickly. We did a study last year across a cohort of AEs and found that the ones using structured pre-meeting intelligence ramped about 41% faster than the control group. What's driving that in your case — is it mostly the preparation quality or something earlier in onboarding?"*

*[Introduce the number, then immediately return the conversation to her. You're not presenting — you're validating her hypothesis and staying in discovery mode.]*

### Suggested Opening

*Rep personalizes this before the call — this is a starting point, not a script.*

"Sarah, thanks for making time — I know the first 90 days in a new role means your calendar is probably overcommitted. I saw you grabbed our rep ramp benchmarks guide a few weeks back, so I'm guessing this is an active area of focus for you. I'd rather spend this conversation understanding what you're seeing at Vantara before I say anything about what we do — we can figure out together whether there's actually a fit here. Can you start by telling me what prompted you to dig into this space right now?"

*[This opening: acknowledges her time, references the inbound signal without being creepy about it, signals that this is a diagnostic conversation not a pitch, and ends with an open question. The last line gives her full control of what comes next.]*
